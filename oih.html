What the chart says

In plain terms: Orascom Investment Holding trades at EGP 1.41 against a four-lens weighted fair value near EGP 0.78 — roughly −45% — so the market has already paid for the Pyramids, and the case for the price is a bull case, not a base case. The primary holding-company NAV, marking every asset at its audited value and putting a DCF on the Giza plateau concession, lands at EGP 0.81; a stricter consolidated DCF is EGP 0.48; only a normalized-earnings view of the concession at maturity (EGP 1.03) approaches the tape. What EGP 1.41 is paying for is three things the accountable books do not yet contain: the Pyramids platform at maturity rather than at its ramp-stage value, some recovery of the ~US$114m of cash trapped in North Korea beyond the 50% the auditors allow, and the Sawiris platform premium Egyptian retail money awards this counter. The chart, meanwhile, is strong — up ~74% in a year and consolidating just under the 50-day — so this is a valuation story fighting a momentum tape, not a cheap stock.

How to read the odds bar

It's a picture of where the price could be — not a single guess. Here's each part:

The thick teal band is where the price lands about half the time. The two numbers above it are the edges of that "middle half."
The thin pale band is almost the whole range — about 9 times out of 10 the price stays inside it. The far numbers are the rare low and rare high.
The upright line is the middle outcome — half the simulations land above it, half below.
The gold dot is the latest price. If most of the bar sits to its right, the odds lean up; to its left, they lean down.

The sentence above the bar says the same thing in words. These are model estimates from 50,000 simulations — useful odds, never guarantees.
