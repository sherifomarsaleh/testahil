Trend, momentum and key levels — what is the price doing now?

What the chart says

In plain terms: Dubai Islamic Bank is the UAE’s largest Islamic bank, earning a 19%+ return on tangible equity, yet it trades at just 1.31× book — cheaper than every UAE bank peer, on one of the best returns among them. It got there in two steps: an 8.6% collapse the session after February’s results, when the dividend was cut from 45 fils to 35, and then the regional war. Five lenses put fair value between AED 8.14 and AED 11.20, weighted central AED 10.18 — and unusually, even the market-anchored relative lens sits above the price, not on it. The honest counter is capital, not valuation: CET1 of 12.6% sits close to the effective 12.5% regulatory floor, the margin is still falling, and the cost of risk has already tripled off a very low base. Squeeze both at once and the buffer thins further, and a second dividend cut becomes the live risk. What moves it near-term is the margin path, the cost of risk, and whether the Iran-war ceasefire — broken and restored more than once already — holds.

How to read the odds bar

It's a picture of where the price could be — not a single guess. Here's each part:

The thick teal band is where the price lands about half the time. The two numbers above it are the edges of that "middle half."
The thin pale band is almost the whole range — about 9 times out of 10 the price stays inside it. The far numbers are the rare low and rare high.
The upright line is the middle outcome — half the simulations land above it, half below.
The gold dot is the latest price. If most of the bar sits to its right, the odds lean up; to its left, they lean down.

The sentence above the bar says the same thing in words. These are model estimates from 50,000 simulations — useful odds, never guarantees.
