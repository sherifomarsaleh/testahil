What the chart says

In plain terms: Apple trades well above our weighted central fair value — richly valued on every fundamental lens. It is the highest-quality cash machine in technology: an iPhone-led hardware franchise (iPhone, Mac, iPad and Wearables) wrapped around a fast-growing, high-margin Services layer (App Store, licensing, iCloud, advertising, payments). On modelled cash flows the consolidated DCF is near $152 and the segment sum-of-the-parts near $184; forward multiples ($249) and normalized earnings ($253) sit higher, and the roughly $90 gap between the two families is the durability premium — the installed base, switching costs and Services annuity the explicit five-year model does not capitalise. The bull case needs Services to keep compounding and an AI-led iPhone upgrade cycle to land; the cautious case is a regulated Services take-rate and flat hardware as the multiple de-rates. Over three months the distribution is near-even on direction with a mild downside tilt from event skew — not a view that the shares are cheap or expensive, just where price could travel from here.

What drives the odds

50,000 paths, 16 forces: the Services/AI-cycle drift, iPhone & hardware volumes, gross-margin mix, Nasdaq/high-beta flows, US real rates and FX — plus event forces: an AI-upgrade/WWDC catalyst, a Services-growth print, a quarterly earnings/margin print, a capital-return refresh, a China demand/share shock, an App Store/antitrust ruling and the Google traffic-acquisition outcome. Zero secular drift (international name); the mild downside tilt is event skew, not a valuation view.

Where Apple sits in its markets

Apple sits within the US mega-cap technology cohort — the highest-quality cash machine in the group but among the slower growers, which is why its forward multiple sits above the cohort median yet below the AI-capex-levered names. Peers trade on different growth-and-margin profiles and aren't directly value-comparable, so this is a competitive map, not a price table.

How to read the odds bar

It's a picture of where the price could be — not a single guess. Here's each part:

The thick teal band is where the price lands about half the time. The two numbers above it are the edges of that "middle half."
The thin pale band is almost the whole range — about 9 times out of 10 the price stays inside it. The far numbers are the rare low and rare high.
The upright line is the middle outcome — half the simulations land above it, half below.
The gold dot is the latest price. If most of the bar sits to its right, the odds lean up; to its left, they lean down.

The sentence above the bar says the same thing in words. These are model estimates from 50,000 simulations — useful odds, never guarantees.
