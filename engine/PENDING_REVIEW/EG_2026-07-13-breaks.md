# PENDING REVIEW — EG (Egypt) — BREAK DEFINITION CORRECTED — 2026-07-13

**Methodology change, not a mechanical refit. It changes the cone width for every EGX name.**

## What was wrong

Sherif: *"First it was from March. Second, devaluation is a way of life in Egypt, even sharp ones."*
Both correct, and together they invalidate the old break list.

1. **The old list missed the biggest devaluation.** It read `["2016-11-03","2022-03-21","2023-01-11"]`
   and stopped at Jan-2023 — omitting **6-Mar-2024**, the largest float in the series
   (EGP ~30.9 -> ~50.2). `apply_breaks` cuts at `MAX(breaks)`, so the live filter cut at
   2023-01-11 and **only worked by accident**: it happened to leave the Mar-2024 jump INSIDE
   the calibration sample.

2. **Devaluation is the process, not a break from it.** Mar-2022, Oct-2022, Jan-2023, Mar-2024
   — roughly one every two years. Filtering them out filters out the risk.

## Why "just fix the list" would have made it worse

Adding 2024-03-06 (the true last break) cuts the sample to a **devaluation-free** period. The
fit obediently thins the tail (nu 4 -> 5) and narrows the cone (cal 0.909 -> 0.850) — and then
**wins the skill test**, because it is scored on a calm sample. The original adoption test walked
straight into this: it compared configs *"both scored on the same post-break windows"* —
circular by construction.

## Evidence (committed 29-name panel)

The column that matters is 90%-band coverage during the windows that actually CONTAIN the Mar-2024 float.

**2022-03-21 is where Egypt's serial-devaluation regime begins.** The pound sat flat near 15.7
for years, then stepped Mar-22 -> Oct-22 -> Jan-23 -> Mar-24. Cutting there keeps THREE
devaluations in the sample: widest cone of any config, best available jump coverage, zero FAILs.
Going back to 2016 drags in the stable managed-float years — a different regime — and it HURTS.
