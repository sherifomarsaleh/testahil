Trend, momentum and key levels — what is the price doing now?

What the chart says

In plain terms: gold pays nothing and has no cash flows, so its value rests on the published analyst consensus (a Reuters poll of 30 analysts centres on ~$4,800 for 2026) and the strongest structural demand in its modern history — record 2025 demand above 5,000 tonnes, central banks buying 863 tonnes, and gold now the largest reserve asset by value. Spot ~$3,990 sits about 5% below the $4,200–5,000 consensus zone after a ~29% correction. The near-term cap is rising real rates and a firm dollar; the floor is the structural central-bank bid.

The five forces — drag to explore

the most decision-relevant of the 11 drivers

There are two kinds of slider. The first three are steady pressures: the number is how much that force pushes gold each quarter — e.g. −2.5% / quarter means it is right now dragging gold down about 2.5% over three months; slide it positive and it lifts gold instead. The last two are one-off events: the number is the chance it happens and the jump if it does — e.g. 30% · +4% is a 30% chance, worth +4% to gold. Either way, dragging right is better for gold, left is worse.

higher-for-longerFed cuts / easing

What it is: how attractive cash and bonds are next to gold, which pays no interest. While the Fed holds rates high (“higher-for-longer”), money prefers bonds and gold slips — the biggest near-term drag, and the reason for the fall off the January high. Drag right (rate cuts) to ease the drag and lift the forecast; left to deepen it.

dollar surgesdollar weakens

What it is: the US dollar’s strength. Gold is bought and sold in dollars, so a stronger dollar makes it pricier everywhere else and cools demand (DXY is above 100, its highest since May-2025). Drag right (weaker dollar) to push the forecast up; left (dollar surge) to push it down.

disinflationreflation

What it is: how fast money is losing value. When inflation runs hot or government debt balloons, people buy gold to protect their savings. Drag right (reflation) to lift gold; left (disinflation) to soften it; the centre is neutral — which is where the study sets it today.

demand fadesrecord buying

What it is: how hard the world’s central banks are buying gold for their reserves — the single largest structural buyer (863 tonnes in 2025). This one is an event: the number is the chance of a fresh buying surge and the +4% it would add. Drag right for a higher chance of record buying; left for fading demand.

calmrisk-off surge

What it is: fear. Wars, crises and market panics send money rushing into gold as a safe haven. This is also an event: the number is the chance of a haven shock this quarter and the +4% it would add. Drag right for a higher chance of a risk-off surge; left for calm.

How to read the odds bar

It's a picture of where the price could be — not a single guess. Here's each part:

The thick teal band is where the price lands about half the time. The two numbers above it are the edges of that "middle half."
The thin pale band is almost the whole range — about 9 times out of 10 the price stays inside it. The far numbers are the rare low and rare high.
The upright line is the middle outcome — half the simulations land above it, half below.
The gold dot is the latest price. If most of the bar sits to its right, the odds lean up; to its left, they lean down.

The sentence above the bar says the same thing in words. These are model estimates from 50,000 simulations — useful odds, never guarantees.
