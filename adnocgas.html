What the chart says

In plain terms: ADNOC Gas trades a little below our weighted central fair value — roughly fairly valued to modestly cheap, and priced less as a growth stock than as a sovereign-backed income instrument yielding about 5%. It supplies about 60% of the UAE’s sales gas under a 25-year take-or-pay contract, so the domestic book is a contracted annuity; the roughly 40% liquids-and-LNG export leg is oil-linked and carries the earnings swing. The gap between our lenses is the story: on the whole enterprise’s cash flow the shares look cheap (DCF ~AED 4.50), but on the dividend the shareholder actually receives they look fair (dividend-discount ~AED 3.41, essentially spot). ADNOC owns about 86% and controls whether the retained cash is ever paid out. What moves it is the Brent-linked export price and whether that gap between enterprise value and the paid dividend ever closes.

The five levers

most decision-relevant of the 16 forces
oil fallsoil rises

The export swing factor. The ~40% liquids-and-LNG leg is priced off Brent, so the barrel over the quarter — OPEC+ policy, demand and inventories — sets the export drift. The ~60% domestic book is contracted take-or-pay and does not move with it.

escalation fearedcalm

A one-off security or facilities event in the region — the kind of incident seen at Habshan in 2025 — that disrupts operations or sentiment. The main near-term downside for an Abu Dhabi energy asset.

nonenew SPA signed

A new long-term LNG or gas sales-and-purchase agreement — Ruwais LNG offtake or a domestic supply deal — that adds contracted volume. An upside catalyst that lengthens the take-or-pay book.

demand softensdemand accelerates

The second-order pull: global gas and LNG demand, spot prices and the volume ramp at Ruwais. A steady drift on the export leg’s realized price.

rates riserates fall

ADNOC Gas trades as a ~5% dividend instrument, so the required yield moves with rates. The dirham’s peg ties the UAE to the Fed; falling real rates lift the value of the dividend, rising rates compress it.

How to read the odds bar

It's a picture of where the price could be — not a single guess. Here's each part:

The thick teal band is where the price lands about half the time. The two numbers above it are the edges of that "middle half."
The thin pale band is almost the whole range — about 9 times out of 10 the price stays inside it. The far numbers are the rare low and rare high.
The upright line is the middle outcome — half the simulations land above it, half below.
The gold dot is the latest price. If most of the bar sits to its right, the odds lean up; to its left, they lean down.

The sentence above the bar says the same thing in words. These are model estimates from 50,000 simulations — useful odds, never guarantees.
