What the chart says

In plain terms: Saudi Aramco trades a few percent above our weighted central fair value — roughly fairly valued to modestly rich, and priced less as a growth stock than as a sovereign-backed income instrument yielding about 5.2%. It sits on a 247-billion-barrel reserve base at the lowest production cost in the world, but the government owns ~81.5% and takes the great majority of the cash through royalties, tax and the dividend. The bull case (~SAR 31) is an oil re-rating and yield compression while the reserve base is credited in full; the cautious case (~SAR 20) is a sustained oil slide that forces the dividend — already running at essentially 100% of free cash flow — to be rethought. What moves it is the oil-price path and whether that base dividend stays covered.

The five levers

most decision-relevant of the 16 forces
oil fallsoil rises

The dominant driver. Aramco’s revenue, cash flow and the dividend’s coverage all move with the barrel. The direction of Brent over the quarter — OPEC+ policy, demand, and inventories — sets the operating drift.

recession fearedbenign

A one-off demand shock — a global slowdown that pulls oil demand and the barrel down together. The main near-term downside for a pure oil producer.

calmsupply shock

A supply disruption — a regional escalation or an outage — that spikes the oil price. As a low-cost producer with spare capacity, Aramco is a net beneficiary of a supply-driven price jump.

demand softensdemand accelerates

The second-order pull: global demand growth, refining and chemicals margins, and the downstream contribution. A steady drift on the barrel and the crack.

rates riserates fall

Aramco trades as a ~5.2% dividend instrument, so the required yield moves with rates. The riyal’s peg ties SAMA to the Fed; falling rates lift the value of the dividend, rising rates compress it.

Where Saudi Aramco sits in its markets

Aramco is an integrated oil major — upstream, downstream and chemicals — and the world’s largest and lowest-cost crude producer. Peers trade in different currencies and aren’t directly value-comparable, so this is a competitive map, not a price table.

How to read the odds bar

It's a picture of where the price could be — not a single guess. Here's each part:

The thick teal band is where the price lands about half the time. The two numbers above it are the edges of that "middle half."
The thin pale band is almost the whole range — about 9 times out of 10 the price stays inside it. The far numbers are the rare low and rare high.
The upright line is the middle outcome — half the simulations land above it, half below.
The gold dot is the latest price. If most of the bar sits to its right, the odds lean up; to its left, they lean down.

The sentence above the bar says the same thing in words. These are model estimates from 50,000 simulations — useful odds, never guarantees.
