What the chart says

In plain terms: Al Rajhi Bank trades a few percent below our weighted central fair value — roughly fairly valued, and the whole argument is what its retained capital is worth. On the dividend it pays today (a rising ~half of earnings) the shares look fairly priced near SAR 58; on the value it builds by reinvesting at a ~23% return, the excess-return lenses land at SAR 76–80. It is the world's largest Islamic bank — a retail-led franchise with the Kingdom's deepest low-cost deposit base, earning ~23% on equity at a sector-low ~23% cost-to-income. The bull case (~SAR 80) is durable margin resilience and a step-up to a 55–60% payout; the cautious case (~SAR 58) is a faster SAMA cutting cycle that squeezes the net interest margin and incremental capital earning closer to the cost of equity. What moves it near-term is the rate path and the quarterly NIM print.

Al Rajhi is the world’s largest Islamic bank and Saudi Arabia’s largest bank by market value — a retail-led franchise earning a ~23% return on equity. It screens richly against Saudi and GCC peers, a premium its returns earn. This is a competitive map, not a price table.

How to read the odds bar

It's a picture of where the price could be — not a single guess. Here's each part:

The thick teal band is where the price lands about half the time. The two numbers above it are the edges of that "middle half."
The thin pale band is almost the whole range — about 9 times out of 10 the price stays inside it. The far numbers are the rare low and rare high.
The upright line is the middle outcome — half the simulations land above it, half below.
The gold dot is the latest price. If most of the bar sits to its right, the odds lean up; to its left, they lean down.

The sentence above the bar says the same thing in words. These are model estimates from 50,000 simulations — useful odds, never guarantees.
