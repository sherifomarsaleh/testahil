What the chart says

In plain terms: Emirates NBD trades about 5% below our weighted central fair value — modestly cheap, and unusually, four independent bank lenses cluster tightly. A dividend-discount / residual-income model lands near AED 33, a discounted-cash-flow (FCFE) build near AED 31, relative multiples near AED 33 and a through-cycle normalized-earnings read near AED 31 — a tight AED 31–33 cluster. Emirates NBD is the UAE’s largest bank and the second-largest in the GCC — assets above AED 1.2 tn, ~56% owned by the Investment Corporation of Dubai, an AED 471bn low-cost (CASA) deposit base that cushions the margin as rates fall, and a ~20% return on tangible equity at a sector-low cost-to-income. The bull case (~AED 43) is durable margin resilience, a benign credit cycle and a step-up in capital returns; the cautious case (~AED 25) is a faster Fed/CBUAE cutting cycle that squeezes the net interest margin and a cost-of-risk normalising off a recovery-flattered ~0.2% trough. What moves it near-term is the rate path, the Dubai credit cycle and the quarterly NIM print.

Peer set & risks

Where Emirates NBD sits in its markets

Emirates NBD is the UAE’s largest bank and the second-largest in the GCC by assets — ~56% owned by the Investment Corporation of Dubai, with a deep low-cost (CASA) deposit base and a large international footprint (DenizBank in Türkiye, the incoming RBL platform in India). It screens mid-pack against Gulf peers despite top-tier returns. This is a competitive map, not a price table.

The debate is valuation, not quality — whether the UAE’s largest bank at ~1.6× tangible book on a ~20% return is too cheap, which all four lenses answer modestly yes. UAE conventional banks trade below the Islamic-premium names (Al Rajhi, ADIB) despite comparable returns. Competitive map, not advice.

How to read the odds bar

It's a picture of where the price could be — not a single guess. Here's each part:

The thick teal band is where the price lands about half the time. The two numbers above it are the edges of that "middle half."
The thin pale band is almost the whole range — about 9 times out of 10 the price stays inside it. The far numbers are the rare low and rare high.
The upright line is the middle outcome — half the simulations land above it, half below.
The gold dot is the latest price. If most of the bar sits to its right, the odds lean up; to its left, they lean down.

The sentence above the bar says the same thing in words. These are model estimates from 50,000 simulations — useful odds, never guarantees.
