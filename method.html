Methodology

Every instrument passes through three independent lenses, then the results are reconciled — agreement raises confidence, divergence is published as-is.

LENS 1

Fundamental

Bottom-up: each project valued from its units (area × price/m² and cost/m²), rolled into full financial statements, then DCF and sum-of-the-parts with each project weighted by its execution probability. Output: a value range (bear / base / full execution) — never one number.

LENS 2

Technical

Three timeframes (weekly / daily / hourly): trend and structure, levels, moving averages, momentum (RSI/MACD), and the decisive referee — whether volume confirms the move or calls its bluff.

LENS 3

Monte Carlo

One model, 50,000 paths, 10 external factors: 5 continuous (policy rate, EGP/USD, inflation, market flows, oil) and 5 events with stated probabilities (geopolitics, sovereign inflows, launch execution, tourism, mortgage credit). Every assumption is a published, criticizable number — the table IS the model.

Why distributions, not price targets?

Because a single number is a comfortable lie. The future is a probability distribution, and whoever sells you certainty sells you an illusion. We publish the median, the bands, and level-touch probabilities — then measure our calibration in public: do the 90% bands actually contain reality 90% of the time?

Momentum-reading rule (educational)

Price rising while RSI stalls or falls = bearish divergence, momentum dying into strength. Price and RSI rising together = healthy trend. Price falling while RSI rises = seller exhaustion worth watching. Both falling together = healthy downtrend, don't catch the knife. Descriptive patterns for reading momentum — not signals to act on.

Where the numbers come from

We separate hard facts from our own estimates, and we label vendor data when we use it:

  • Company filings & audited reports — the Egyptian Exchange (EGX) disclosures, annual and quarterly financial statements, for revenue, backlog, debt, cash and segment detail.
  • Central Bank of Egypt (CBE) & CAPMAS — interest rates, inflation, and FX, used in the calculator and the Monte Carlo factors.
  • Price history — daily closing prices from market data vendors, used for the technical read and the simulation's volatility. Flagged as vendor data.
  • Sell-side notes (e.g. EFG Hermes, HSBC) — used only as an external cross-check on our own value, always cited and dated.
  • Our own estimates — every assumption in the model (discount rate, sold/unsold split, execution probabilities, escalation, exit multiples) is ours, stated openly, and editable in the downloadable Excel.

Audited figures are distinguished from estimates inside each model. When a number is a vendor figure or a best-effort reconstruction, we say so.
